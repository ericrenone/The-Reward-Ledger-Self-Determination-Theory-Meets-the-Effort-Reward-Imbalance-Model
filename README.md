# The Reward Ledger: Self-Determination Theory Meets the Effort-Reward Imbalance Model

*Why feeling intrinsically driven by your work doesn't, by itself, protect you from the cost of an unbalanced trade.*

Two frameworks in psychology speak directly to what happens when people pour themselves into demanding, meaningful work. One explains why that work feels good. The other explains what happens to your health when the trade underneath it stops being fair — even if the work still feels good.

## The case for intrinsic drive

Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan) is one of the most thoroughly replicated frameworks in motivational psychology, with more than five decades of research and meta-analyses spanning education, healthcare, sport, and the workplace. Its core claim: when three psychological needs are met — autonomy (a sense of choice rather than coercion), competence (a sense of being effective), and relatedness (a sense of connection to others) — people show more engagement, more persistence, and better well-being. Work that's chosen, skill-appropriate, and socially connected is reliably good for people. This part of the story is well established.

## The other half of the ledger

Johannes Siegrist's effort-reward imbalance (ERI) model starts from a different premise: workplace social exchange. People give effort under an implicit reciprocity norm — the expectation that effort will be matched by adequate reward. The model defines reward broadly, across three channels: money, esteem, and status control (job security and promotion prospects). Effort is split into two parts — the extrinsic demands of the job itself, and a separate trait called over-commitment, a pattern of excessive work-related striving and difficulty disengaging, regardless of what the job is actually asking for.

When effort chronically outweighs reward, the model predicts measurable harm, and this is one of the more heavily validated findings in occupational health research: hundreds of studies across many countries link this imbalance to elevated risk of coronary heart disease, depression, burnout, and dysregulated stress physiology. A 2026 three-decade retrospective by Siegrist himself reaffirms the same pattern researchers have found since the model's introduction — a consistent, moderately elevated risk of ischemic heart disease and depression tied to effort-reward imbalance — while also noting that the model needs continued refinement to keep up with how work has changed (gig work, remote arrangements, blurred boundaries between job and identity).

Over-commitment, notably, doesn't just amplify the imbalance — it predicts worse outcomes on its own, including burnout and abnormal cortisol patterns, independent of whether the job's external demands are actually high.

## Where the two frameworks actually meet

This is the part that matters and rarely gets said out loud: the trait that the effort-reward model treats as a risk factor — over-commitment, the inability to disengage — can look, from the outside and from the inside, exactly like the deep intrinsic motivation that self-determination theory says is good for you. Autonomous, competence-driven, meaningful engagement and maladaptive over-investment are not the same thing, but they produce similar behavior: working past the point a detached observer would call reasonable, because the work itself supplies the motivation rather than anything external.

Intrinsic motivation can soften the felt cost of being underpaid, under-recognized, or insecure in a role — people genuinely driven by autonomy and meaning report more resilience under strain. What it does not do is cancel the physiological cost. The cardiovascular and depression risk tracked by the effort-reward model shows up regardless of how the person describes their motivation. Loving the work is not the same as being adequately repaid for it, and the body keeps score on the second thing whether or not the mind is keeping score on the first.

## What actually follows from this

The two literatures point to a conclusion that's a little less comforting than "just find meaningful work." Meaning and autonomy are protective, but only as one side of a trade that still needs its other side honored — real recognition, real security, real proportionality between what's given and what comes back. And the personality pattern most likely to keep someone in an imbalanced trade without complaint — the one that looks, from inside, like passion or purpose — is the specific pattern the occupational health literature flags as the most dangerous to ignore.
